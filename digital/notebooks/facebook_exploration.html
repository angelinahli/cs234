What do I sound like online? - Me vs. My Facebook Friends.¶

A machine-learning driven exploration of online speech patterns¶

In this project, I want to explore several questions about my online speech patterns, as well as the speech patterns of other people. I'll be asking five main questions:

  1. What words do I use that differentiate myself the greatest from my friends over Facebook Messenger?
  2. Which people do I talk to on Facebook Messenger - and how frequently do I talk to each of them? Do I talk to some significantly more than others?
  3. Do I use different words to talk to different people on Facebook?
  4. How do I express myself differently via e-mail as opposed to via Facebook Messenger?
  5. Do the emails I send contain more words on average than the messages I send on Facebook?

To answer these questions, I'll be using two primary source of data:

  1. Facebook messenger data
  2. GMail message data

In this notebook, I'll be exploring my Facebook messenger data. I'll attempt to answer the first three questions:

  1. What words do I use that differentiate myself the greatest from my friends over Facebook Messenger?
  2. Which people do I talk to on Facebook Messenger - and how frequently do I talk to each of them? Do I talk to some significantly more than others?
  3. Do I use different words to talk to different people on Facebook?

def get_best_features(vectorizer, classifier, n=30):
    feature_names = vectorizer.get_feature_names()
    # coefficients are for the words I most distinctly use as opposed to others
    assert len(classifier.coef_[0]) == len(feature_names)
    coefs = sorted(zip(classifier.coef_[0], feature_names), reverse=True)
    
    print("coef\tword")
    print("============")
    for i in range(n):
        coef, word = coefs[i]
        print("{}\t{}".format(round(coef, 4), word))

That's really interesting - it suggests that perhaps, subtle differences in the frequencies with which I use seemingly 'generic' words are really what characterize my speaking style online, as compared to the speaking styles of my friends.

One possibility for why this classifier might be performing poorly is that my speaking style (and the speaking style of my friends) might be changing significantly over time, such that the 'time trends' in my speaking habits might make it very difficult for the classifier to pick out any specific words that differentiate me from others.

Let's try to 'control' for this by restricting our classification process to within each year.

def get_overall_df(list_of_messages):
    """
    Given a list of message dictionaries (in the form of the
    json files we've been working out of), will return a formatted,
    shuffled dataframe of all messages.
    """
    df = pd.DataFrame(list_of_messages)
    df["time"] = df.time.apply(parse_time_string)
    df["label"] = df["from"].apply(
        lambda name: "me" if name == "Angelina Li" else "not me")
    df = df.sample(frac=1, random_state=2).reset_index(drop=True)
    return df

def get_train_test_data(df):
    all_text = df["text"].tolist()
    all_labels = df["label"].tolist()
    
    halfway = int(len(all_text)/2)
    train_text = all_text[:halfway]
    train_labels = all_labels[:halfway]
    test_text = all_text[halfway:]
    test_labels = all_labels[halfway:]
    
    return {
        "train_text": train_text,
        "train_labels": train_labels,
        "test_text": test_text,
        "test_labels": test_labels
    }

def get_accuracy(vectorizer, classifier, data):
    test_vector = vectorizer.transform(data["test_text"])
    accuracy = 0
    for i, actual_label in enumerate(data["test_labels"]):
        case = test_vector[i]
        predicted_label = classifier.predict(case[0])[0]
        if predicted_label == actual_label:
            accuracy += 1
    return {
        "accuracy": accuracy,
        "n": len(data["test_text"]),
    }

def get_accuracy_top_features(df, get_data, top_features=30):
    """
    Given a shuffled dataframe, will print information about
    the accuracy of the Multinomial NB classifier on these data,
    as well as the most predictive features the classifier has access to.
    
    df: The dataframe over which to create training and testing data from.
    get_data: Function that takes in a dataframe and returns a dictionary
    of training and testing data.
    """
    data = get_data(df)
    
    vectorizer = CountVectorizer()
    x_train = vectorizer.fit_transform(data["train_text"])
    
    classifier = MultinomialNB()
    classifier.fit(x_train, data["train_labels"])
    
    acc = get_accuracy(vectorizer, classifier, data)
    print("ACCURACY\n========")
    print("{} out of {} correct, with an accuracy rate of {}%.\n".format(
        acc["accuracy"], 
        acc["n"], 
        round(100*acc["accuracy"]/acc["n"], 3)
    ))
    
    get_best_features(vectorizer, classifier, top_features)

b. Classification by thread¶

We could also look specifically at the differences in the speech patterns between me and a specific person, in case my speech patterns differ wildly between different conversation threads.

Here, Facebook makes life easy for us - each message in our dataframe already comes with a thread_id denoting a conversation with any given person.

We can find the people I talk to the most on Facebook, and see what speech patterns separate myself from my conversation partners.

First, let's find the people I talk to the most on Facebook.

from collections import Counter

def get_top_threads(n, main_df):
    thread_counts = Counter(main_df["thread_id"].tolist())
    return thread_counts.most_common(n)

def get_top_conversations(n, main_df):
    top_threads = get_top_threads(n, main_df)
    top_individuals = {}
    for thread_id, count in top_threads:
        match_id = main_df["thread_id"] == thread_id
        match_label = main_df["label"] == "not me"
        temp_df = main_df[match_id & match_label]
        name = temp_df["thread_members"].iloc[0]
        top_individuals[thread_id] = {
            "name": name, 
            "count": count}
    return top_individuals

Notice that our segments by thread classifications also seem slightly more accurate than our general classifier - again, this makes sense under our given rationale because controlling for different individuals I have conversations with implicitly sets impositions on time lengths as well - for instance, 2 out of the 5 threads I've seen the most conversations in were started only after my sophomore year of college (2016) - thus implicitly controlling for some of the changes in my speaking style across time.

5. Stripping out common words¶

From all the analysis we've done so far, it seems like one of the big problem with our classifier is that it is getting confused based on irrelevant, relatively common words.

What might help us parse out the truly unique words that I use as compared to other people, is if we first cleaned our data of some common English words.

To accomplish this, I'll use a list of the top 10,000 English words as determined by Google's Trillion Word Corpus. Thanks to first20hours for making this list open source. I'll parse out the top 500 words from all the text in my messages.

def strip_common_words(s):
    all_words = re.sub("[^a-zA-Z\d\s]", "", s).split()
    strip_text = lambda word: re.sub(regex_match, "", word, flags=re.IGNORECASE)
    stripped = map(strip_text, all_words)
    all_words = list(filter(lambda s: s != "", stripped))
    return " ".join(all_words).strip()

6. Examining our basic classifier¶

I think, besides a genuine lack of features that set my speaking style online apart from the speaking style of my friends, that there might be two alternative reasons why our classifier might be underperforming:

  1. Each individual message contains too little information to be classified.
  2. Our sample size is too small - we should instead use the entire population of Facebook messages we have access to.

Let's test both of these assumptions!

a. What if each message is too short?¶

If the real problem here is that each individual message is too short to be classified in full, we can instead create testing data by randomly generating artificial 'messages' from our overall text messages of varying lengths and seeing whether longer messages fare significantly better under our classification model.

We can also plot the OLS relationship between the length of each message we've classified so far and the likelihood it was classified correctly. If there is a significantly positive relationship between length of message and a correct classification, this might suggest that our classifier is not equipped to deal with small chunks of text.

Let's implement the first test first, and feed our classifier a "bag of words" to train and test.

def get_labelled_bag_words(text, labels):
    labelled_bag = {}
    
    for i, text in enumerate(text):
        label = labels[i]
        if label not in labelled_bag:
            labelled_bag[label] = []
        labelled_bag[label].append(text)
        
    for label in labelled_bag:
        labelled_bag[label] = " ".join(labelled_bag[label])
    
    return labelled_bag

def get_train_test_words(df):
    all_text = df["text"].tolist()
    all_labels = df["label"].tolist()
    
    halfway = int(len(all_text)/2)
    train_text = all_text[:halfway]
    train_labels = all_labels[:halfway]
    
    test_bag = get_labelled_bag_words(all_text[halfway:], all_labels[halfway:])
    test_text = list(test_bag.values())
    test_labels = list(test_bag.keys())
    
    return {
        "train_text": train_text,
        "train_labels": train_labels,
        "test_text": test_text,
        "test_labels": test_labels
    }

def make_sample_test_data(text, labels, len_message, num_samples=500):
    """
    This function will artificially create random 'messages' made up of
    words selected randomly from each bin. It will generate an approximately
    equal number of messages for each label category.
    """
    new_labels = []
    new_text = []
    
    assert len(text) == len(labels)
    old_data = zip(labels, text)
    
    for l, t in old_data:
        text_words = t.split()
        for _ in range(int(num_samples/2)):
            new_msg = " ".join(random.sample(text_words, len_message))
            new_text.append(new_msg)
            new_labels.append(l)
    return {
        "test_labels": new_labels,
        "test_text": new_text
    }

def get_len_accuracy_dict(df):
    """
    Returns a dictionary plotting the average accuracy of a classifier for each text length.
    """
    data = get_train_test_data(df)
    
    vectorizer = CountVectorizer()
    x_train = vectorizer.fit_transform(data["train_text"])
    
    classifier = MultinomialNB()
    classifier.fit(x_train, data["train_labels"])
    
    test_vector = vectorizer.transform(data["test_text"])
    
    len_accuracy = {}
    
    for i, actual_label in enumerate(data["test_labels"]):
        text_len = len(data["test_text"][i])
        
        case = test_vector[i]
        predicted_label = classifier.predict(case[0])[0]
        correct = 1 if predicted_label == actual_label else 0
        
        if text_len not in len_accuracy:
            len_accuracy[text_len] = []
        len_accuracy[text_len].append(correct)
    
    for text_len in len_accuracy:
        len_accuracy[text_len] = np.mean(len_accuracy[text_len])
    
    return len_accuracy

8. Do I talk to different people differently online?¶

Previously, the question we were mostly focused on is whether I talk differently from other people online. I think it'd also be interesting to look at my speech patterns when talking with different friends, and see whether or not there are significant differences in the way I talk to some people as compared to others.

In this section, I'll pull data from the five friends I talk to the most online, and will see whether or not a Multinomial Naive Bayes classifier can distinguish between whether I talk to each friend in a different way.

def get_friends_train_test(main_df, top_friends=5):

9. Concluding reflections¶

A basic takeaway from this project has been that the Multinomial Naive Bayes Classifier performs relatively poorly in the context of this kind of text based analysis. Even after all of the ways I've attempted to troubleshoot this model, the highest accuracy I achieved in classifying my messages was only 66.444% - still barely better than average.

Nevertheless, I still learned several interesting things about my habits on Facebook messenger:

  1. A surprising fact I uncovered through this project was the fact that I seem to spend a very significant amount of time talking to very few people. The person I talk to by far the most online makes up around 17.234% (91703/532108) of my overall online interactions.

  2. Most of the words that seem to best predict whether a text message is from me as opposed to from someone else are surprisingly mundane. The top 10 features (run on the entire population) are: the, to, you, it, and, that, is, of, in, we. On the one hand, this seemingly random set of words might serve to prove the point that a "bag of words" construct is a poor representation of actual speech, that loses some of the nuances of when we use words and for what reason. On the other hand, it's interesting to think that perhaps there are subtle differences in the frequency with which I use certain words (which I am not even aware of myself) are really what distinguishes me from other people online.

  3. It seems like I do "code switch" when talking to different people online in subtle ways.
