10.25.2017

It was interesting to read the article "Science Isn't Broken" and to reflect on the use of statistical methods in academic publications. I think the most striking thing about this article was how differently the public and academics perceived published studies. While it widely accepted amongst academia that, because of the p<=0.05 cutoff point many academic journals use to determine which results are considered statistically significant, at least 1 in 20 published findings are likely not significant, it seems like the general public tends to perceive published studies as the absolute truth.

The central takeaway of the article is that, despite valid and serious concerns about p-hacking and other techniques that undermine the legitimacy of some published work, academic research as a whole can still make progress by relying on careful peer reviews and meta-analyses of important questions that give less weight to any one flawed study.
